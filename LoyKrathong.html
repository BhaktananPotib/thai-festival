2. Loy Krathong

From:newthaihotels.com (Microsoft bing)

Loy Krathong is one of Thailand’s most beautiful and meaningful festivals. The word “Loy” means “to float,” and “Krathong” is a small, decorated basket usually made from banana leaves, flowers, candles, and incense. The festival is celebrated every year on the full moon night of the 12th lunar month, usually in November, when rivers, canals, and lakes are illuminated with thousands of glowing krathongs.

From:blogspot.com (Microsoft bing)

The main purpose of Loy Krathong is to pay respect to Phra Mae Khongkha, the goddess of water, for providing life and sustenance. People float krathongs on the water to symbolically release misfortune, negative thoughts, and bad luck. It is also a way to ask for forgiveness from nature and to seek blessings, happiness, and good fortune for the coming year.

Loy Krathong is celebrated throughout Thailand, but some of the most famous events take place in Bangkok, Sukhothai, and Chiang Mai. In Chiang Mai, Loy Krathong coincides with the Yi Peng Lantern Festival, when thousands of lanterns are released into the sky, creating a magical scene that symbolizes hope, luck, and new beginnings.

From:stock.adobe.com (Microsoft bing)

In addition to floating krathongs, people often participate in cultural performances, beauty contests, and parades. Families and friends gather to enjoy the festive atmosphere, reinforcing bonds and sharing joy. Loy Krathong is therefore not only a spiritual and cultural event but also a celebration of community and togetherness.

From:Oleg Prachuk (Microsoft bing)

In conclusion, Loy Krathong is a festival that combines beauty, tradition, and spiritual meaning. By floating krathongs and releasing lanterns, people show gratitude to nature, let go of past misfortune, and welcome blessings for the future. It remains one of Thailand’s most cherished and visually stunning festivals.
